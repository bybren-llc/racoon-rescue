## STORY CHECKLIST → `/story-check` or Agent Enhancement

12 questions to ask about your script:

1. What are the elements of my story? (concept, genre)
2. What do I want to say? (theme, message)
3. How will audiences react? (interesting, believable, compelling)
4. Where does my story begin? (setup, crisis, world, rules)
5. Where does my story end? (resolution, questions answered)
6. Are there rising tensions? (surprises, escalation, climax)
7. Who are my characters? (likable, interesting, unique)
8. What is my protagonist like? (empathy, wants, stakes)
9. What are my characters' motivations? (shown, justified)
10. Who/what is the antagonist? (worthy foe, drives, wants)
11. Do I over-explain? (show don't tell, events on screen)
12. Are fictitious characters believable as real?

## HOOK/LOGLINE STRUCTURE → `/logline`

Parts of the Hook (per WTFB):
- **Characters**: Who are they? What do they want? Goals?
- **Conflict**: Main obstacle to achieving goal?
- **Setting**: Time period, genre, style
- **Action**: Excitement, never be boring!

Examples:
- "An archeologist is hired by the U.S. government to find the Ark of the Covenant before the Nazis." (Raiders)
- "A Roman general is betrayed and his family killed by an insane emperor. Now a gladiator, he comes to Rome for revenge." (Gladiator)

## NARRATIVE TYPES → Agent Reference

For story-analyst to identify:
- **Linear**: Beginning to end chronologically
- **Non-Linear**: Not chronological (Memento)
- **Multi-Narrative**: Multiple points of view
- **Dual**: Two perspectives or two time periods
- **Fragmented**: Non-linear sequence
- **Metafictive**: Breaking fourth wall
- **Personal**: Autobiography, biopic

## BEAT USAGE → Dialogue-Writer Enhancement

Per WTFB:
- Beat = pause or brief moment of silence
- Use `(beat)` parenthetical sparingly
- For heavy beats, show action instead:
  ```
  SAL: Magoo, how the hell are you?
  Magoo chuckles apprehensively.
  SAL (cont.): Whoa, aren't you supposed to be dead?
  ```

## FLASHBACK GUIDANCE → Scene-Writer Reference

- Powerful cinematic tool for:
  - Sending back through time
  - Re-visiting historical events
  - Setting up back-story
  - Explaining present actions
- Avoid making it cheesy
- If it doesn't propel story, don't use it

## REWRITING PHILOSOPHY → Standards-Reviewer Enhancement

WTFB rewriting approach:
1. Ask "What are the stories?" - list sentences starting with "The story of..."
2. Chart the arcs across acts (beginning, act one, act two, act three, finish)
3. Find HOLES in the chart
4. Grade scenes (A, B, C, D, F)
5. Make every scene as good as your best scene
6. Compression is artful - shorten everything

## TREATMENT FORMAT → Template

Per WTFB, include:
- Title of movie
- Your name and contact details
- Logline (2-3 lines)
- Explanation of Act One (where, what, who, tone, setup)
- Explanation of Act Two (conflict, change, direction)
- Explanation of Act Three (resolution, climax)
- Wrap-up (character fates, epilogue, sequel potential)
- Write like present-tense short story, NOT novel
- Focus on key plot points, tone, genre
- Leave out unimportant subplots

## SYNOPSIS REQUIREMENTS → `/synopsis`

Per WTFB, a good synopsis must:
1. Be easily pitchable by producer to others
2. Be instantly understandable on first read
3. Give accurate picture of the story
4. Be one page or less
5. Not over-complicate with every character/detail
